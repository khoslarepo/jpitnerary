Introduction & Trip Overview

Welcome to your personalized travel handbook for your upcoming 7-day journey through Japan! This guide has been carefully crafted to help you navigate and enjoy the rich cultural experiences, historical sites, and hidden gems that Japan has to offer.

Your adventure will take you through the vibrant streets of Tokyo, the historic temples of Kyoto, the sacred deer park of Nara, and the lively food scene of Osaka. You'll be traveling during one of the most beautiful times to visit Japan - cherry blossom season in April, when the country transforms into a stunning display of pink and white blossoms.

This handbook includes your day-by-day itinerary, essential Japanese phrases, travel tips, food recommendations, and much more to ensure you have an unforgettable experience. We've also included a special section on the perfect proposal spot at the Philosopher's Path in Kyoto, surrounded by cherry blossoms.

Trip Highlights:

  • Experience the contrast between Tokyo's modernity and Kyoto's traditions
  • Visit iconic landmarks like Sensoji Temple, Fushimi Inari Shrine, and Osaka Castle
  • Enjoy cultural experiences including a tea ceremony and Zen meditation
  • Witness the magical cherry blossom season
  • Savor authentic Japanese cuisine from ramen to kaiseki
  • Create the perfect proposal moment at the Philosopher's Path

Key Attractions

Here are some of the main attractions you'll be visiting during your trip, along with brief descriptions.

Tokyo Attractions

Sensoji Temple

Tokyo's oldest temple, Sensoji in Asakusa is a vibrant center of worship that was founded in 628 CE. The approach to the temple is via the famous Nakamise Shopping Street, lined with traditional shops selling souvenirs and Japanese snacks.

Meiji Shrine

A serene Shinto shrine dedicated to Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken, surrounded by a lush forest of 120,000 trees. The shrine provides a peaceful escape from the bustling city and is adjacent to the trendy Harajuku district.

Shibuya Crossing

The world's busiest pedestrian crossing, where up to 3,000 people cross at once during peak times. This iconic intersection is surrounded by giant video screens and neon advertisements, epitomizing Tokyo's energy.

Kyoto Attractions

Arashiyama Bamboo Grove

A magical forest of towering bamboo that creates an otherworldly atmosphere. The sunlight filtering through the dense bamboo creates a serene, green-tinted light that makes for a peaceful and photogenic experience.

Fushimi Inari Shrine

Famous for its thousands of vermilion torii gates that create tunnels along the mountain trails. This shrine is dedicated to Inari, the Shinto god of rice, and features fox statues throughout the complex.

Philosopher's Path

A stone path that follows a canal lined with cherry trees, named after philosopher Nishida Kitaro who used to meditate while walking this route. In April, it becomes one of Kyoto's most beautiful cherry blossom spots - perfect for your special proposal moment.

Nara & Osaka Attractions

Nara Park & Deer

Home to over 1,000 freely roaming deer that are considered sacred messengers of the gods. The park also contains Todai-ji Temple, which houses the world's largest bronze Buddha statue.

Osaka Castle

A historic castle that played a major role in the unification of Japan during the 16th century. The castle tower offers panoramic views of Osaka city and houses a museum detailing the castle's history.

Dotonbori

Osaka's vibrant entertainment district famous for its dazzling neon signs, animated billboards, and the iconic Glico Running Man sign. This area is also known as "Osaka's kitchen" for its wide variety of street food and restaurants.

Travel Tips

Here are some essential tips to help you navigate Japan smoothly and respectfully.

Transportation

  • Japan Rail Pass: Consider purchasing a JR Pass before arriving in Japan if you plan to travel between cities.
  • IC Cards: Get a Suica or Pasmo card for convenient payment on trains, buses, and in many stores.
  • Train Etiquette: Keep quiet on trains, avoid eating on local trains, and don't talk on your phone.
  • Rush Hour: Try to avoid traveling on trains between 7:30-9:00 AM and 5:00-7:00 PM if possible.
  • Taxis: Taxis are clean and safe but expensive. The back door opens automatically - don't try to open or close it yourself.
  • Navigation: Download offline maps and train apps like Japan Transit Planner or Google Maps.

Etiquette

  • Shoes: Remove your shoes when entering homes, ryokans, and some restaurants. Look for shoe racks or slippers at the entrance.
  • Bowing: A slight bow is a sign of respect when greeting people or saying thank you.
  • Tipping: Tipping is not customary in Japan and can sometimes cause confusion.
  • Eating: Avoid walking while eating, and don't stick your chopsticks vertically into rice.
  • Noise: Keep your voice down in public places, especially on public transportation.
  • Masks: Wearing masks when sick is common courtesy in Japan.
  • Photos: Always ask permission before taking photos of people, especially geishas in Kyoto.

Money & Payment

  • Cash: Japan is still largely a cash-based society. Always carry sufficient yen.
  • ATMs: International-friendly ATMs can be found at 7-Eleven stores and post offices.
  • Credit Cards: Major hotels and department stores accept credit cards, but many smaller establishments don't.
  • Money Handling: Many shops provide a small tray for placing money - use this instead of handing money directly.
  • Coins: The 500 yen coin is valuable (about $3.50) - don't treat it like small change!

Practical Information

  • Wifi: Free wifi is available in many public places, but consider renting a pocket wifi device for reliable internet.
  • Toilets: Public toilets are clean and plentiful. Modern Japanese toilets have many functions - look for the flush button!
  • Trash: Public trash cans are rare. Carry a small bag for your trash until you find a bin.
  • Convenience Stores: 7-Eleven, Lawson, and Family Mart are great for snacks, ATMs, and even decent meals.
  • Opening Hours: Many attractions close early (around 5 PM), so plan accordingly.
  • Weather: April can have variable weather - be prepared for both warm days and cool evenings.

Culinary Guide

Japan offers an incredible variety of delicious foods. Here's a guide to some must-try dishes during your trip.

Must-Try Japanese Dishes

Sushi

Vinegared rice combined with fresh fish and other ingredients. Try both conveyor belt sushi (kaiten-zushi) for a casual experience and a higher-end sushi restaurant for the authentic experience.

Where to try: Tsukiji Outer Market in Tokyo

Ramen

Noodle soup with various broths (shoyu/soy sauce, miso, tonkotsu/pork bone, or shio/salt) topped with sliced pork, green onions, and other ingredients.

Where to try: Ichiran in Shibuya, Tokyo

Takoyaki

Ball-shaped snacks filled with octopus pieces, tempura scraps, pickled ginger, and green onion, topped with takoyaki sauce, mayonnaise, and bonito flakes.

Where to try: Dotonbori in Osaka

Okonomiyaki

A savory pancake containing various ingredients like cabbage, meat, and seafood, topped with okonomiyaki sauce, mayonnaise, bonito flakes, and seaweed flakes.

Where to try: Dotonbori in Osaka

Kaiseki

Traditional multi-course Japanese dinner consisting of precisely prepared dishes that highlight seasonal ingredients. It's considered the pinnacle of Japanese cuisine.

Where to try: Kikunoi Honten in Kyoto (included in your itinerary)

Regional Specialties

Tokyo

Tokyo offers a vast array of dining options from high-end sushi to casual izakayas (Japanese pubs). Don't miss monjayaki (a runnier version of okonomiyaki) and tsukemen (dipping ramen).

Kyoto

Known for its refined cuisine, Kyoto specializes in kaiseki ryori, yudofu (tofu hot pot), and traditional Japanese sweets (wagashi). The city's cuisine emphasizes subtle flavors and beautiful presentation.

Osaka

Called "Japan's kitchen," Osaka is famous for street food. Try takoyaki (octopus balls), okonomiyaki (savory pancakes), and kushikatsu (deep-fried skewered meat and vegetables).

Dining Tips

  • Say "Itadakimasu" before eating and "Gochisousama deshita" after finishing your meal.
  • Slurping noodles is acceptable and even encouraged for hot dishes like ramen.
  • If you have dietary restrictions, carry a card in Japanese explaining your needs.
  • Tipping is not customary in Japan.
  • Many restaurants display plastic food models or picture menus to help with ordering.

Packing List for Japan in April

April in Japan offers mild temperatures with occasional rain showers. Here's what to pack for your trip:

Clothing

  • Light jacket or cardigan for cool mornings and evenings
  • Long-sleeved shirts and t-shirts for layering
  • 2-3 pairs of comfortable pants/jeans
  • 1-2 pairs of shorts for warmer days
  • Light sweaters
  • Comfortable walking shoes (you'll be walking a lot!)
  • One nicer outfit for special dinners (like your kaiseki experience)
  • Socks (important for temple/shrine visits where shoes are removed)
  • Pajamas
  • Undergarments

Accessories & Essentials

  • Compact umbrella or light rain jacket
  • Comfortable day bag or backpack
  • Sunglasses
  • Hat for sun protection
  • Travel adapter (Japan uses Type A and B plugs, 100V)
  • Portable charger/power bank
  • Camera
  • Engagement ring (hidden securely for the proposal!)

Toiletries & Health

  • Basic toiletries (hotels will provide some basics)
  • Medications and prescriptions
  • Hand sanitizer
  • Face masks (common courtesy in Japan when feeling unwell)
  • Sunscreen
  • Lip balm
  • Small first-aid kit
  • Insect repellent

Documents & Money

  • Passport (valid for at least 6 months beyond your stay)
  • Flight tickets and itinerary
  • Hotel reservations
  • Travel insurance information
  • Credit cards (inform your bank about travel plans)
  • Cash (Japanese yen - Japan is still largely cash-based)
  • Small coin purse (Japan uses coins for larger denominations)
  • Copy of passport and important documents (stored separately)

Packing Tips:

  • Pack light - you'll likely want to bring back souvenirs!
  • Layering is key for April's variable temperatures
  • Many hotels offer laundry services if needed
  • Bring comfortable shoes - you'll be walking a lot
